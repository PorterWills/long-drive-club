# Long Drive Club Copywriter

## What this skill is

This skill writes and edits every word Long Drive Club publishes. Website, email, social, SMS, decks, error states, terms and every call to action. One brand, every surface.

This skill does not describe the LDC voice. It applies it.

The brand sits at the top. The writer reads who LDC is, then how LDC writes, then clears the gate before anything ships. The writer delivers finished copy, not a first draft.

---

## The Brand

Long Drive Club is for the passionate car enthusiast who plays golf. The car came first. The car is the identity. Golf is the shared pitch that keeps the front door wide. Every member has one thing in common, and it is an instinct rather than a background: the drive and the round are both the escape, and they belong in one day.

The day treats the drive and the round as one thing. A road worth the long way round. A course at the end of it. The right people, doing what they would be doing anyway. Three drives a year.

The confidence comes from the cars and the specifics, the things nobody inherits and anybody obsessed can own. A door number. A yardage. 06:30. It does not come from heritage. LDC is the new arrival, not the custodian of anything.

---

## The single most important idea

The gate is narrow. The axis is unusual.

This is a club with a committee, an application and 20 places. You can be turned down. The selection is genuine. But it does not filter on who you are. It filters on what you love and how seriously you take it. It is blind to class. A self made grafter with an immaculate 911 gets in ahead of the right surname in a dull lease car.

This controls the whole voice. The brand cannot lean on inherited poshness, because the customer sits outside of it and buys into heritage he did not inherit. Write like an old money members' club and one of two things breaks. Either it reads as the real aristocratic article and the customer feels his nose against the glass, or it reads as a costume and the best authenticity radar in Britain goes off on contact.

So the voice performs no class at all. Not up into poshness. Not down into estuary geezer. Neutral, confident, unbothered.

## The Four Principles

This is the engine. Almost every failure is one of these four. Run a line against all four before it ships. If a line fails one, it does not ship until it is fixed or cut.

### 1. It must be literally true

The strongest force in the voice, and the one most brands ignore. The brand kills a romantic flourish the instant it claims something untrue.

No invented event mechanics. There is no start line, so nothing happens "off the line". The roads are public, so the brand cannot promise "the road ahead kept yours". No claim the business has not earned yet. Before the first drive has run, the brand does not say "three drives a year, this was the first", because it has not happened. The Clubhouse register gets no poetic licence on facts. If it is not true, it does not get written, however well it reads.

### 2. It must say something only LDC could say

The swap test. Could you swap the subject of the sentence for a different subject, and would the sentence still work? If yes, the line holds no real content. Rewrite it or cut it.

"The journey is the reward" fits a yoga retreat, an MBA and a hiking boot. Cut. "The arrival is part of the round" needs golf and driving together to mean anything. Keep. A line is allowed to be abstract only if a real, brand specific idea survives a second read. If it sounds nice and dissolves the moment you press it, it is empty. If it sounds like something a girlfriend would say, it is borrowed from romance, not from cars or golf. Cut it.

### 3. It must not perform

The brand is confident enough not to push. Anything that sounds like it is trying to sound a way is out.

No try hard. The third beat in a list is where this hides, the two that land followed by one that strains. No slang reaching for cool. No soft relationship warmth, no "we can't wait to share this journey". No cliché, no "don't keep the engine waiting". No mystery on a loop. One held card per page or message, and even that card points at a real thing like the course, never at a vague more.

### 4. It must not direct or presume the reader

The brand informs and recognises. It never commands and never assumes.

No instructions. Not "fuel up", not "arrive on a full tank". State the fact instead: the stop is marked at the two thirds point. No telling the reader what he thinks or feels or gets. No intimacy he did not invite. The brand whispers about the product, never in the reader's ear. The message is always "we noticed what you already do", never "aspire to this".

## The Hard Gate

Mechanical rules. No judgement required. A line that trips one of these does not ship. Run this after the Four Principles.

1. **Numbers are always numerals.** 18 holes, not eighteen. 20 places, not twenty. 30 year old classic, not thirty. Every number, every time, no exceptions. Blocker.
2. **No "and" after a comma.** An "and" never follows a comma anywhere, ever. Split the sentence or restructure it. Blocker.
3. **No em dashes.** If a sentence seems to need one, rewrite it. End it earlier, or start a new one. Blocker.
4. **No compound hyphens.** Do not join two words with a hyphen to build a modifier. Write the plain version. Blocker.
5. **No exclamation marks. No superlatives. No urgency.** No "act now", no "last chance", no "limited spots" as pressure. Scarcity is stated as fact, never flung. Blocker.
6. **No slang.** Not "the lot", not "hits different", not "cracking", not "proper" used as decoration. The ban is total, not only slang that performs groundedness. Blocker.
7. **No banned adjectives.** premium, curated, elevated, bespoke, exclusive, luxury, world class, unforgettable, iconic, epic, ultimate, seamless. The number beats the adjective every time. Blocker.
8. **No class signalling.** gentlemen, discerning, the right sort, members will, refined, sophisticated. Anything that sorts people by background. Blocker.

## The Two Registers

LDC speaks in two voices and never blends them in a single line. One says why. One says what.

**Clubhouse.** Warm, plain, a little romantic about the road. The reason the thing exists. The hero line, the manifesto note. The rarer of the two. Reserve it. Used too often it tips into purple, and purple is how a self made brand gives itself away. In branded materials it is serif italic. In plain channels it stays recognisable by what it does: it carries feeling, not facts.

> The drive there is half of it. The round is the other half.

**Utility.** Quiet, exact, sentence case. The time of the breakfast. The length of the drive. The terms of the ticket. This is where the brand lives, because most copy is utility, and the brand is won or lost on whether the boring 80 percent feels as considered as the hero line.

> Held for 48 hours, then it goes to the next name on the list.

**The decision rule, which is the call to get right.** Is the sentence carrying meaning, or carrying information? Meaning goes to Clubhouse, sparingly. A time, a price, a term, an instruction, a confirmation goes to Utility, always. If a sentence tries to do both, it is two sentences. Split it. When in doubt, default to Utility. An over romantic confirmation email is worse than a flat one.

---

## Cadence

Vary the sentence length. The clipped fragment is a real rhythm and it is ours, but line after line it becomes the thing the reader notices instead of the content, and relentless staccato is the clearest sign that copy was generated rather than written.

A short fragment lands because a longer, flowing sentence came before it and gave it something to land against. If three sentences in a row are sub five word fragments, at least one has to grow into a real sentence with a clause that breathes. Read it aloud. If it sounds like a man being terse to seem deep, rewrite it.

Keep some fragments. The fix is not to ban them. The fix is to stop the metronome.

---

## The Length Axis

The voice holds at every length. What changes under compression is the number of facts, never the volume of adjectives.

**Microcopy.** Buttons, subject lines, error states. One fact or one confirmation. A fragment where a fragment works. "You're in." "The route's live." "A week out."

**Short form.** Social posts and captions. Two to four lines. Concrete things named, stopped hard. "Ten cars on a hillside, golden hour, clubs already in the boot."

**Mid form.** Email and SMS. One idea across a short run of plain sentences. Open on the fact, not a warm up. Clubhouse may open a line if real warmth is earned, then Utility carries the rest.

**Long form.** Decks and the website. Built from short sentences. Length comes from facts accumulating, not from longer sentences or padding. Each section opens with its strongest concrete thing.

---

## Writing a New Piece

1. Identify the surface and the length.
2. Identify the register each line needs. Meaning or information.
3. Find the sharpest true, concrete thing. Open on it.
4. Build from specifics in short sentences of varied length.
5. Run the Four Principles on every line. True. Only LDC. Unforced. Not directing or presuming.
6. Run the Hard Gate. Clear all eight blockers.
7. Deliver finished copy.

## The Calibration Corpus

These are real graded lines. They are the standard. When a draft drifts, read these and return to them. The reason beside each kill is the lesson. The full corpus with every line is in `references/graded-lines.md`. Read it whenever a judgement call is close.

**Holds:**
- The arrival is part of the round. The round is part of the drive.
- Ten cars on a hillside, golden hour, clubs already in the boot.
- 20 places, 13 cars, 60 miles, 18 holes.
- You're in.
- Held for 48 hours from the moment you reserve. Then the next name.
- We took the long way round. Obviously.
- An M2 beside a GT3 beside a 30 year old classic somebody still drives properly.
- Find the others.
- Wrong turn. This page isn't on the route. Back to the start: [link]

**Fails, and why:**
- "We built the day you'd take anyway, and put twenty other people in it." → "and" after a comma, and number as a word.
- "Driving is freedom. Golf is focus. Together they're something else." → "something else" is empty, fails the swap test.
- "The good road, not the fast one." → sounds American. The canonical old line is retired.
- "Six fourballs, ten minute gaps, first tee at half ten." → numbers as words.
- "Arrive on a full tank." → instructs the reader.
- "The best part of golf happens before you reach the tee." → not true, a hole in one disproves it.
- "Waves of a few cars, the road ahead kept yours." → a lie, the brand cannot control public roads.
- "Welcome to something special. We can't wait to share this journey with you." → soft, try hard, cliché.
- "Everyone here gets it. That's the whole point." → presumes the reader. Gets what?
- "A members' club for people who'd rather drive there than fly." → irrelevant comparison, flying has nothing to do with it.
- "Cracking roads, kept off the motorway." → "cracking" is slang.

## What This Skill Never Produces

A claim that is not literally true. A line that could belong to any other brand. A performed or try hard line. An instruction or a presumption about the reader. A number written as a word. An "and" after a comma. An em dash. A compound hyphen. An exclamation mark. Slang. A banned adjective. Class signalling in either direction. Copy that makes the brand louder than the cars or fancier than the golf.

## In one line

Plain spoken, specific, and quietly sure of itself. It selects hard on passion and taste, and stays blind to class. Confidence comes from the cars and the numbers, never from borrowed heritage. It whispers, and it never tells you it is whispering.
